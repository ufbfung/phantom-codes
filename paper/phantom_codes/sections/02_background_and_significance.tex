\section{BACKGROUND AND SIGNIFICANCE}\label{background-and-significance}

Medical concept normalization is the entity-linking task of mapping
in-text mentions of biomedical concepts like diagnoses, medications,
labs, and procedures to entries in a standardized ontology or
controlled vocabulary such as ICD-10-CM, LOINC, or RxNorm
\autocite{Miftahutdinov2021,Xu2022}. It sits at the foundation of
healthcare data interoperability. Errors propagate downstream: a
misassigned diagnosis code can shift a hospital's case-mix index,
mis-stratify a research cohort, or quietly corrupt the training data
of the next generation of models. For most of the last decade, the
state of the art for automated coding came from supervised neural
classifiers trained on labeled clinical text
\autocite{Mullenbach2018,Huang2022}. The arrival of general-purpose
large language models has changed the deployment economics:
foundation models can in principle perform code prediction with no
task-specific fine-tuning. The questions that motivated this work
are not whether LLMs can predict medical codes --- they can --- but
whether the safety findings from earlier model generations still
hold on the frontier models shipping today, and at what cost the
resulting deployment economics actually pencil out.

Two gaps in the literature shape what this paper is for. First,
\textbf{frontier-model evaluations age fast}. Soroush et al.\ (2024)
reported that GPT-4 achieved only 46\% exact match on ICD-9 and 34\%
on ICD-10, with a substantial fraction of errors being non-existent
codes rather than real-but-wrong assignments \autocite{Soroush2024}.
Lee and Lindsey documented that LLMs systematically struggle to
distinguish real codes from plausibly-formatted fakes
\autocite{Lee2024}. These findings frame the safety risk that
fabricated codes pose, an instance of the broader medical-
hallucination phenomenon \autocite{Kim2025,Ji2023}, but both are
$\sim$18 months old. In that window Anthropic shipped Opus 4.7,
OpenAI shipped GPT-5.5, and Google shipped Gemini 3 Flash Preview.
Whether the safety story holds for the current generation is an
empirical question no published evaluation has yet answered.

Second, \textbf{deployment cost is mostly absent from the
literature}. The field's response to hallucination has largely been
architectural: retrieval-grounded pipelines
\autocite{DasBaksi2024,Sarvari2025}, agentic walkers
\autocite{Motzfeldt2025}, neuro-symbolic verifiers
\autocite{HybridCode2025}, and broader benchmark expansions
\autocite{Almeida2025,Singh2025,Li2025,Gershon2025}. Across this
body of work, hallucination is rarely treated as a first-class
outcome and per-prediction cost is rarely reported at all. As LLM-
augmented coding moves toward production deployment, the
deployment-decisive question shifts from ``which model is most
accurate'' to ``which (model, prompting mode) combination is Pareto-
optimal in accuracy and cost.'' The literature does not currently
support that question.

We present \textbf{Phantom Codes}, a reproducible benchmark that
contributes on three fronts. \emph{First}, we evaluate the most
recent generation of frontier LLMs: 24 (model, prompting-mode)
configurations spanning Claude Opus 4.7, Sonnet 4.6, and Haiku 4.5;
GPT-5.5 and GPT-4o-mini; and Gemini 2.5 Pro, 2.5 Flash, and 3 Flash
Preview --- many shipped within weeks of evaluation. Where prior
work reports findings on 2023--2024 vintage models, this matrix
tests whether those findings still hold. \emph{Second}, we treat
per-call cost as a first-class outcome alongside accuracy. Every
prediction in the matrix records token usage and dollar cost at
runtime, computed against the published per-token rates of each
provider; we report cost-per-correct (USD per exact-match outcome)
as the deployment-relevant normalization and identify the small
Pareto frontier in the accuracy-cost plane an operator should
actually deploy. \emph{Third}, we provide methodological instruments
that make the above two contributions land cleanly: a six-way
outcome taxonomy in which fabrication of non-existent codes
(hallucination) and abstention (\texttt{no\_prediction}) are
explicit, mutually exclusive buckets; a within-model ablation across
zero-shot, constrained, and retrieval-augmented prompting modes
that holds the model and input fixed and varies only whether a
candidate list is provided; and an abbreviation-stress mode (D4)
that replaces canonical display strings with clinical jargon (T2DM,
HTN, CKD-3) to expose the condition under which string-matching
baselines collapse. The headline matrix runs entirely against
Synthea-generated FHIR Bundles \autocite{Walonoski2018}, a freely-
redistributable synthetic patient dataset that gives full
reproducibility from a pinned Synthea version and seed.
