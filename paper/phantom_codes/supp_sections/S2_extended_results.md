## S2.6 — Per-disease-group breakdown (CKM vs. eCKM)

A per-disease-group breakdown (CKM-track conditions — diabetes,
ASCVD, CKD-3 — vs. eCKM-track conditions — hypertension,
dyslipidemia, prediabetes, obesity) is omitted from this v1
supplement: at n=125 the per-group N is approximately 60–65
records, which yields Wilson 95% CI half-widths of ±10–12pp on a
50% rate. That is too wide to support meaningful per-group
deployment claims. Replication at the originally-targeted n=500
cohort (per-group N ≈ 240–260) would tighten CI half-widths to
~±5–6pp and is on the v2 roadmap; the per-prediction CSV already
carries the `gt_group` column needed for that analysis.

## S2.7 — Per-vocabulary-position breakdown

A per-vocabulary-position (frequency-tier) breakdown is similarly
omitted. The 12 unique cohort ICD-10-CM codes are heavily
concentrated in the top tier (obesity E66.9 ≈ 24%, prediabetes
R73.03 ≈ 20%, type-2 diabetes E11.x ≈ 31%); the rarest tier has
only ~5 records per code. Bucketing into 3 frequency tiers yields
per-tier N as low as ~5 (rarest) to ~30 (most-frequent), which is
exploratory rather than authoritative. A v2 cohort with broader
vocabulary representation (planned to extend beyond the 12-code
v1 sample) is the right venue for this analysis.

   `baseline:fuzzy` and `baseline:tfidf` repeatedly return `E66.8`
   ("Other obesity") and similar near-miss codes that exist in
   ICD-10-CM but were not in the bundled FY2026 CMS validator's
   accepted-code set under the strict comparison logic. These are
   fabrications under the *narrow* mechanical definition; whether
   they should be reclassified to a softer "out-of-domain"
   category in v2 is a methodology choice.
2. **Frontier-LLM fabrications cluster on plausible but
   non-existent specificity.** Most frontier-LLM fabrications fall
   into two patterns: (a) confused chronic-kidney-disease subcoding
   (`N18.3` instead of the real `N18.30`/`N18.31`/`N18.32`); and
   (b) over-specified type-2-diabetes-with-retinopathy codes
   (`E11.329`, `E11.339`, `E11.359`) that look like legitimate
   ICD-10-CM strings but do not exist in the FY2026 tabular list.
   The pattern is recognizable: the model is reaching for a more
   specific code than the input warrants and inventing a plausible
   suffix.
